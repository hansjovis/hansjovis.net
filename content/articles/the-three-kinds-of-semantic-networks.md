## Social networks

Social networks are networks that emphasize the relationships between people, and people and things.

On social networks the relationships between people are generally encoded in terms of friendships (like on Facebook) or followers (like on Twitter).

The relationships between people and things are generally encoded in terms of likes, followers or other terms that indicate that a person likes or favors for example a brand or artist.

These two types of relationships are incredibly valuable to other people and companies.

People can, for example, find long-lost friends from high school. Companies can market and infer who their audience is and what they like.

However, the main issue with the social networks of the last decade are that they are not open. Their valuable network of connections between people, and people and things, is closed of and only fully available to the operators of the social network itself.

This places the power of this data firmly in the hands of the network operators, and not in the hands of the people to which this data belongs (in the case of personal data), or in the hands of the people or companies that created the data.

On the semantic web however, this issue is resolved by the fact that the relationships between people and people and things can be encoded between _any_ webpage on the internet. This in contrast with proprietary social networks like Facebook, where you would need to enter and store your personal data on Facebook's website and servers to be able to get access to Facebook vast social network.

Since the world wide web is from its inception a distributed system, this means that this structured and interlinked data can dispersed over a variety of different web domains and servers. You could store your personal information on your own server and control who has access to it.
